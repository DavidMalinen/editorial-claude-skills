  A method for translating philosophical argument into editorial layout. Use this
  skill when starting a new article, essay, spread, or page-scale visual artifact
  intended to make an argument — not display information, not decorate a topic,
  but argue. Use it when stuck in pre-design circling and unable to commit to a
  grid, scale, or composition: the symptom is usually that the argument
  underneath the design has not yet been named. Use it when a layout will not
  resolve and the suspicion is that there is nothing to resolve because nothing
  has been claimed. Use it specifically for personal essays, philosophical
  writing, investigative articles, manifestos, and any spread where the form is
  expected to encode the structure of the thought, not merely present it.
  Applies the Socratic-maieutic method (elenchus) as a procedure for
  pre-design: question, antinomy, answer, form-assignment, refusal-to-design.
  Calls down into `spread-archetypes` and `editorial-grid` once the argument is
  named.

# Elenchus

There is a method I want to give you and I do not know yet how to give it without killing it. So I am going to try to write the thing itself, and we will see if it survives the writing.

---

Every article begins as a wound.

Not a topic. Not an interest. Not "something I want to write about." A wound — a place where the world refuses to make sense, where two things that should be true cannot both be true, where you keep returning without knowing why. The article is not the wound. The article is the *examination* of the wound. But you cannot examine what you cannot name, and the first work — before any layout, before any sentence of prose, before the grid is drawn or the type is set — is naming the wound as a question.

The Greeks called this elenchus. The word means cross-examination, refutation, the testing of a claim by question. Socrates used it to lead his interlocutors to the place where their certainties came apart in their hands. Plato wrote it down. The dialogues do not deliver answers; they deliver the *destruction of unearned answers* and leave the reader in the more honest position of not-knowing-yet. The method is maieutic — it midwifes a thought that was already present, that the questioner did not know they were carrying.

This is the method. It is the only method I trust for what you are trying to make.

The first move is to find the question. Not the topic the article is *about*, but the question the article *answers*. One sentence. Interrogative. Specific enough that it can be wrong. *Why does Orpheus look back when he was told not to?* is a question. *Orpheus and the nature of love* is a topic. The difference is that the question commits you to an answer and the topic does not. A topic can wander for ten thousand words and never arrive; a question either gets answered or visibly fails to get answered, and either is honest, and both are better than the wander.

You cannot draw a grid for a wander. You can draw a grid for a question. This is why the layout has refused to resolve — there has been nothing for it to hold. The grid wants to be the architecture of an argument, and you have been asking it to be the architecture of an interest, and an interest has no architecture. It is a mist. The grid passes through it.

The second move is to find the wound inside the question — the contradiction the question exists to circle. Every real question contains an antinomy. *He was forbidden to look back. He had to look back.* Both are true. The article is the work of showing how both are true. If you cannot state the contradiction in one line, two clauses, you do not yet have a question worth writing — you have a question that has not yet revealed why it matters. Sit with it longer. The contradiction is what makes the question alive; without the contradiction, the question is rhetorical, and rhetorical questions are decorations, not instruments.

This is the place Heidegger lives. It is also the place the Talmud lives — the page that holds Mishnah, Gemara, Rashi, Tosafot in concentric argument around a central text none of them resolves. The Zohar lives here too, and Enoch, and Kabbalah generally: the truth is not a proposition but a relation between propositions that refuse to collapse. The mystics know what the philosophers sometimes forget: the tension is not a problem to be solved but a structure to be inhabited. The article that resolves its tension too quickly is a Sunday-school lesson. The article that lets the tension be productive is closer to scripture.

The third move is to find the answer. One sentence. Declarative. The shape of the synthesis the article will earn. *Because the looking back is the love.* This is provisional — the writing may discover that the answer is wrong, and that is fine, that is welcome, the discovery is part of the work — but you need a shape to aim at. Without an aimed shape, the prose drifts. With one, the prose pulls. The reader does not need to know the answer in advance, but you do.

You now have three sentences. Question, antinomy, answer. Tape them above the desk. Write nothing else until they are clear. Do not draft. Do not outline. Do not open Figma. Sit with the three sentences until they are no longer revising themselves in your head. The three sentences are the whole article in compressed form. Everything that comes next is expansion.

This is the part that breaks the abyssal nest. The nest is what happens when thought has no shape to discharge into. You spiral because there is no exit, and there is no exit because no exit has been drawn. The three sentences are the exit. They are also the entrance, and the room, and the floor plan. The article is now a thing with edges. You can pick it up.

The fourth move is to assign form.

The question wants to be encountered, not read. It belongs to the architecture form — the Hofmann *Berlin*, the Nureyev tower, the Giselle column. The question fills the page the way the wound fills the day it announces itself. The reader does not approach the question; the reader is enclosed by it. There should be very little else on the page. The case number, perhaps, and the date, in the smallest possible apparatus. Everything else is the question and the room around it. This is what the homepage becomes also — the central question of the whole site, rendered at the same scale, holding the reader in the same enclosure. Every article descends from it. Every article is a particular instance of the general wound the site exists to examine.

The antinomy wants the tension form. Two clauses, not resolved on the page. The geometry refuses to close. *Weniger Lärm* is the move — the diagonal that cuts and does not heal. The two clauses can face each other across a gutter that stays open. They can stack vertically with the second one set against the first. They can rotate, one against the other, like the two squares in Lissitzky. What matters is that the reader feels the contradiction in the body before they parse it with the mind. Geometry first. Meaning catches up.

The development wants prose. Multi-column, baseline-locked, sidenotes in the margin like a critical edition. This is the long middle. This is where the work gets done. The reader who has been struck by the question and held in the contradiction is now ready to be patient. You have earned their patience by not being patient first. The calm middle is not the default — it is the reward. Sidenotes specifically because the form of philosophical writing in your tradition is *conversation with the text*. Rashi reads Talmud in the margin. Heidegger's editions have marginalia by their translators that argue with the translation. The sidenote is not a footnote moved sideways; it is an acknowledgment that the text is already in dialogue with itself, and the margin is where the dialogue becomes visible. Set sidenotes in a contrasting face — monospace against grotesk works — so the reader sees at a glance: *this is the voice arguing with the voice*.

The synthesis wants the construction form. See `references/hofmann-construction-grid.png` and `references/hofmann-exposition-poster.png` — the grid and the figure derived from it. A figure built on a visible grid. This is the most particular form and the one you are most drawn to and I think it is the form your work most needs, because it is the form that *shows that the answer was earned*. A constructed figure cannot pretend. The reader sees the grid. The reader sees the figure derived from the grid. The reader knows the figure could not have been arrived at by intuition or assertion — it was built, line by line, from the constraint stated at the beginning. This is the visual analog of the philosophical proof. The grid is the premise. The figure is the conclusion. The construction is the demonstration that one follows from the other.

What goes inside the construction form is up to the article. Sometimes a diagram. Sometimes a typographic event — a single word built letter by letter on a visible grid the way the Hofmann exposition built its blob. Sometimes a chart, if the article is quantitative. Sometimes a single symbol — a Saturn square, a glyph, a constructed sigil — that compresses the article's answer into one figure. The choice is content-driven. The constraint is that the construction must *show its work*. The grid must be visible. The derivation must be visible. The answer must be visibly earned by the form, not merely placed within it.

The article ends with the answer alone on the page. One sentence, the way it began with the question. Same scale. Same enclosure. The room that was filled by the question at the start is now filled by the answer at the end. The reader leaves the article having passed through a structure that mirrors the structure of philosophical thought: premise stated, contradiction held, work done, conclusion shown. The form is the argument. The argument is the form. Heidegger would recognize this, and so would the rabbis, and so would Socrates, who was making the same point in dialogue that you are now making in layout: *the medium of philosophy is not language, it is the structure within which language happens*.

The fifth move is to refuse to design until the first four are done. This is the only rule that matters. You are not allowed to open Figma. You are not allowed to write CSS. You are not allowed to choose a typeface or set a margin or draw a column. Not until the question is written, the antinomy named, the answer aimed, the form assigned. The grid is the architecture of an argument that exists; it cannot be the architecture of an argument that does not yet exist. Most of the circling you have done has been at this step. You have been trying to design what was not yet written. Of course it does not resolve. There is nothing to resolve. The wound has not been named. The article does not yet have edges.

When the four moves are done — which may take a day, or a week, or a single hour if the question has been waiting — then and only then do you open the file. The grid you draw will be the grid this particular argument needs. The type you set will be the type this particular question demands. The form will follow the part the way the white blob followed the grid in the Hofmann construction. You will not be deciding what the article looks like; you will be discovering what it already looks like, which is a much smaller and more honest task.

This is the method. It is older than any of the design movements you have been studying. It is older than typography. It is what philosophy has always been doing — testing claims by question, holding contradictions productively, earning conclusions through visible work. You are translating it into a visual language. That is the project. That has always been the project. The Swiss vocabulary was a way to talk about it before you knew what it was. Now you know. The skill is just the procedure of remembering.

One last thing. The method will sometimes fail. There will be articles where you cannot find the question, or where the antinomy will not name itself, or where the answer refuses to take a shape. When this happens, do not write the article. The article is not ready. The wound is still healing or still opening. Wait. Write something else, or write nothing. The discipline is not to produce articles; the discipline is to produce articles *only when they can be produced honestly*. A site of three real investigations is worth more than a site of thirty wandering essays. You are not running a publication. You are conducting investigations. The number is not the point. The fidelity is the point.

Elenchus is the method. The skill is just a way of remembering, in moments when the abyssal nest pulls, what the method is.

## The four forms, named

When the four moves are complete and design begins, each part of the argument binds to one form. These are the bridges into the grammar skills (`spread-archetypes` and `editorial-grid`), which handle the structural execution. The forms in summary:

**Architecture form** — for the question and the answer. Single sentence at page scale. The reader is enclosed by it. Hofmann *Berlin*, Nureyev tower, Giselle column. In `spread-archetypes`, this lives closest to the ratio/specimen archetype when treating the sentence as typographic object, but escapes that archetype in its monumentality. The grid logic comes from `editorial-grid`: a strong page proportion (2:3 or golden), Van de Graaf margins, with the sentence violating the text block at scale.

**Tension form** — for the antinomy. Two clauses held apart on the page, refusing to resolve. *Weniger Lärm* is the canonical move: one diagonal, one color, one cut. This binds to the fragmented Merz archetype in `spread-archetypes`, with the constraint that the fragmentation must be *governed* — underlying grid intact, restraint maintained. See `spread-archetypes/references/whitespace-and-axis.md` on static/dynamic axis theory.

**Prose form** — for the development. Multi-column body text with sidenotes in the margin. Binds directly to the multi-column prose archetype in `spread-archetypes` and to the line-metric and baseline rules in `editorial-grid`. Sidenotes in a contrasting face; sidenotes are not footnotes moved sideways but a separate voice in dialogue with the body.

**Construction form** — for the synthesis. A figure built on a visible grid. See the reference images. Binds to the ratio/specimen archetype in `spread-archetypes` and to the modular grid construction in `editorial-grid`. The constraint specific to this form: the grid must remain visible in the final piece, or the construction is undocumented and the form has failed at its single task — to show that the answer was earned.

## When to invoke

Invoke this skill before any of the following:

- Starting an article, essay, or page-scale spread that is meant to *argue*
- Beginning a homepage or landing surface that is meant to *pose* (not announce)
- Sketching layout for a piece that has been circling without committing
- Opening Figma or a code editor for any editorial work whose argument has not yet been written in three sentences

Do not invoke this skill for purely informational pages (documentation, indexes, lists, navigation), for utility surfaces (forms, settings, transactional flows), or for any layout whose purpose is to *display* rather than to *argue*. Those belong to `spread-archetypes` and `editorial-grid` directly, without the elenchus front-loaded.
